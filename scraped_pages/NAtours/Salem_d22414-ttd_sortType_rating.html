Salem has a dark past, and haunted history tours are an entertaining—and educational—way to learn about the town’s witch trials and other sinister episodes. Whether you are looking for a jump scare or a slight startle, Salem tours serve up the right dose of spook.

Q:Is Salem a walkable town?
A:

Yes, Salem is a walkable town. Most of the major sights are not too far apart from each other, and you can reach most of them by walking from the train station and sticking to a 2-mile radius. There are sidewalks and it’s not very hilly, but some streets are cobblestone so be aware if you have mobility issues.

Q:How much do Salem tours cost?
A:

Salem tours aren’t too expensive. The cheapest ones start at around US$5 for self-guided, downloadable audio tours. Most themed walking tours cost between US$15 and US$55, depending on length and group size. Boat tours cost about US$60 and a witch broom making workshop is US$120.

Q:What do you see on Salem tours?
A:

On Salem tours the focus is typically witches, ghosts, and the town’s haunting history. You’ll likely see attractions like Salem Old Town Hall, Essex Street Pedestrian Mall, Mcintire Historic District, Old Burying Point Cemetery (one of the oldest burial grounds in the country), and the supposedly haunted Turner’s Seafood at the Lyceum Hall. You may also visit the Peabody Essex Museum and the House of Seven Gables, a 1668 mansion featured in a story by Nathaniel Hawthrone.

Q:How many days in Salem is enough?
A:

You should aim for at least two days in Salem. Within two days, you can see landmarks like the House of Seven Gables, Salem Old Town Hall, the Peabody Essex Museum, the Salem Halloween Museum, and all of the sites associated with the city’s famous witch trials in the 1600s. You’ll also have time to discover Salem’s maritime history, with a visit to Pickering Wharf, the Salem Maritime National Historic Site, and a historic schooner ride.

Q:What is the best month to visit Salem?
A:

While many people will want to visit Salem in October to experience the full effect of the town’s spookiness around Halloween, the crowds, lines, and traffic on Halloween itself and the few weeks leading up to it are extreme—and hotels book up way in advance (and at higher prices). Visiting Salem in June, July, August, or September offers ideal weather and smaller crowds.
